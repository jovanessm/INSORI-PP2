Suggested Structure
1. Einleitung / Introduction (~10% of paper)

Why digital sovereignty matters for the EU right now
The core tension: cloud dependency vs. sovereign control
Your research question and thesis


2. Grundlagen / Background (~15%)

What "digital sovereignty" means (define it clearly — it's a contested term)
How cloud computing works and why states/institutions rely on it
Key actors: hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) and their market dominance


3. Wie wird die digitale Souveränität durch Cloud Computing verletzt?(~20%)

Technical lock-in (APIs, proprietary formats, data gravity)
Legal exposure — especially the US CLOUD Act and its extraterritorial reach over US-based providers
Economic dependency: EU market share held by non-EU providers
Strategic/political risks (geopolitical leverage, data access by foreign states)


4. Bestehende Ansätze / Existing Approaches (~25%)

GAIA-X — the EU's federated cloud initiative (and its limitations/criticisms)
European Cloud providers (OVHcloud, Hetzner, IONOS) — viable alternatives?
EUCS (EU Cloud Certification Scheme) — setting sovereignty standards
Data Act & GDPR as regulatory levers
Sovereign cloud offerings by hyperscalers (e.g. Microsoft Azure sovereign clouds) — are these enough?


5. Kritische Bewertung / Critical Analysis (~20%)

None of the existing approaches fully resolve the tension
GAIA-X criticism: too slow, too fragmented, industry-captured
Regulatory approaches don't eliminate technical dependency
The realism argument: full technological independence may be neither feasible nor desirable


6. Lösungsansätze / A Way Forward (~15%)

Layered sovereignty: distinguish between data sovereignty, operational sovereignty, and strategic sovereignty — you don't need all three equally
Open-source & interoperability standards to reduce lock-in
EU investment in domestic cloud capacity (like the US CHIPS Act logic applied to cloud)
International partnerships with like-minded democracies


7. Fazit / Conclusion (~5%)**

Restate the core argument
Sovereignty doesn't mean autarky — it means managed dependency with credible exit options
Policy recommendations in brief


A framing tip: The most compelling argument you can make is probably that sovereignty is a spectrum, not a binary. The goal isn't to kick AWS out of Europe — it's to ensure the EU has enough leverage, legal protection, and alternatives that it is never coerced by its dependencies. That framing makes the paper analytically sharp and avoids naive tech-nationalism.
